# Fed rate hike crisis pattern

Since the 1980s, each major Fed tightening cycle that pushed policy rates above ~5% has been followed—often after the first cuts—by significant financial stress, usually starting in US **periphery** markets before sometimes hitting the US core.

## Historical episodes

| Peak hike era | Rate peak (approx.) | First cuts | Subsequent stress |
|---------------|---------------------|------------|-------------------|
| Early 1980s | ~20% | 1982 | Latin American debt crisis |
| Late 1980s | 9.75% | 1989 | Japan asset bubble burst (1990) |
| Mid 1990s | 6% | 1995–96 | Asian financial crisis (1997) |
| Late 1990s | 6.5% | During cycle | Dot-com bust (2000) |
| Mid 2000s | 5.5% | 2007 | Global financial crisis (2008) |
| Current | 5.25–5.5% (Sep 2023) | Sep 2024 (−50bp) | Window 2024–2027 (TBD) |

## Why this cycle may differ

- **Crisis locus shifted over time:** Latin America → Japan/Asia → US tech (2000) → US housing + Europe sovereign debt (2008–2012).
- **Parallel to 2000:** Technology narrative (AI vs internet) with large productivity claims.
- **Unlike 2000:** Bubble not necessarily punctured *during* hikes; speaker expects **hawkish communication** instead of aggressive further hikes.
- **China angle:** Still exposed to global volatility; domestic regulators have added buffers since prior cycles.
